Meeting the Pieces

Pawn

Pawns can only move 1 space forward, unless they are on their starting square. In that scenario, the pawn can move 2 spaces forward. They are worth 1 point of material.

Knight

Knights are only piece that can jump over other pieces. They move 2 spaces in one direction, and then one in an adjacent direction. They are worth 3 points of material.

Bishop

Bishops can move any amount of squares diagonally. This means that each side has a light and dark squared bishop. They are worth 3 points of material.

Rook

The Rooks can move any amount of spaces horizontally or vertically. They are worth 5 points of material.

Queen

The Queen combines the movement of a rook and bishop, making her the strongest piece on the board. The Queen is worth 9 points of material.

King

The King is the most important piece on the board. It can move 1 square in any direction.

Setting Up the Board

  1. Place the board so that the dark corner squares on each player's left side.
  2. Place the Rooks on the corner squares.
  3. Place the Knights beside the Rooks.
  4. Place the Bishops beside the Knights.
  5. Place the Queen on the same color square on the back rows.
  6. Place the King on the Remaining Square beside the Queen and Bishop.
  7. Place the pawns in front of the pieces.

Captures

Pieces capture by moving onto another piece using their standard movement. Pawns are the exception to this rule. They can only capture 1 square forward diagonally.

Chess Notation

Chess notation has a simple formula to follow, and is important when translating moves. The formula is the piece followed by the square coordinate it goes to. The Coordinates go from A-H (The Files/Rows) and from 1-8 (The Ranks/Columns). a1 is White's dark square corner and h8 is White's dark square corner.
Each piece is represented by a single letter:

  • King: K
  • Knight: N
  • Queen: Q
  • Bishop: B
  • Rook: R

If two pieces of the same type and color can move onto the same square, we also need to specify which piece. This is done by using the file or rank the moving piece is on. We can also indicate captures using 'x'. We can also denote a check with '+' and checkmate with '#' at the end of the move. Finally, Castling is represented with O-O (Kingside/Short) and O-O-O (Queenside/Long). "1. e4 e5" Means that on the first move, White moved a pawn to e4 and Black moved a pawn to e5. "2. Nf3 Nc6" means that White moved a knight to f3 and Black moved a knight to c6. "Ncxd5" means that a knight on the c file captured a pawn or piece on d5.

Check and Checkmate

The King is the most important piece in the game and can not be captured. "Check" refers to a state where the the King is being attacked. "Checkmate" refers to a state where the King can not get out of Check. Checks can be handled by moving the king, capturing the attacking piece, or blocking the check. Because Kings can not be captured, a King can not move into a Check because they would be captured on the next move.

Stalemate and Drawing

There are a multitude of ways to draw the game. One of which is called Stalemate. A Stalemate happens when one side can not make a move that doesn't result in a Check, thus can not make a move, resulting in a draw. There is also the draw by repition. This happens when the same position and same side to move is reached 3 times over the course of the game. There is also the 50 move rule. If a pawn move or pice/pawn capture has not happened in the last 50 moves, the game ends in a draw. The game can also end in a draw if both sides lack sufficient material to force a checkmate. Finally, players can agree to a draw.

Castling

Castling is represented by the notations O-O and O-O-O. Castling happens by moving the King two squares towards the rook, and then moving the rook adjacent to the king and on the other side of the king. You can not castle with a rook if the rook or king has moved. Also, you can not castle through check or while in check.

En Passant

En Passant means "In Passing". In chess, if a pawn moves forward 2 squares, but could have been captured by another pawn if it only moved forward 1 sqaure, it can still be captured by the enemy pawn for 1 move only.
