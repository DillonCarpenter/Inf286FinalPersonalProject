The French Defense

General Plan

The French Defense is one of many reputable options for Black against e4, and it is my personal pick. There are many variations White can respond with, but in general Black gives up space for the ability to attack the pawn on d4 by keeping the pawn structure fixed. This can be done with first c5 and then Nc6. White will likely reinforce their pawn structure with c3 and f4. The only variation that deviates heavily from this plan is the Exchange Variation. However that is fairly simple to play and both sides have a few plans to choose from. One notable variation I am ommitting is the Winawer as it's too advanced for beginners. Additionally, there is the Two Knight's and La Bourdonnais variations that are also omitted.

Pros

  • Clearly defined middlegame plans
  • Less likely for other beginners to have preparation
  • Fairly Aggressive

Cons

  • Less Space
  • Bad Lightsquare Bishop

Classical Steinitz Variation

The Steinitz Variation is a prime example of what the French Defense is. In this variation, there are multiple ways of continuing after 7. Be3. The diagram shows the 4 most popular ways of continuing. That being said, I can not recommend Qb6 as it is a very forcing line for both sides. Study that on your own. There are a few things to keep in mind. For one, Black will need to find a spot for their lightsquare bishop, either b7 to support the d5 pawn or a6 to fight against White's lightsquare bishop. Secondly, the direction of the fixed pawns ensure that White will expand on the Kingside and Black will expand on the Queenside. Thirdly, White should not castle Queenside against 7. Be7. The diagram shows how that situation can develop and how Black can gain initiative if that mistake is made. Finally, there are some lines where White can sacrifice their lightsquare bishop for the pawn on h7. This is known as the Greek Gift.

Advanced Variation

The Advanced Variation is similar to the Steinitz variation, however the knight on g8 needs to find a new home. This will either be on g6 where it is safe and can later be used to attack on the kingside or on f5 to attack the d4 pawn. The most sound moves on move 5 is Qb6, Bd7 and Nh6. Nh6 is the most awkward to play so I do not recommend it. For White, there are a few ideas. For example, routing the knight on b8 to a6 and then c7 helps defend the d4 pawn. D7 is also a fine square. The Diagram goes over some Qb6 and Bd7 lines. Additionally, if White faces the move Nh6, capturing the knight with the dark square bishop is okay, but Black's dark square bishop becomes a lot stronger.

Tarrasche Variation

The Tarrasche Variation is a popular response at higher levels, and has many responses from black as early as move 3. For that reason, this outline focuses on the mainline which starts with 3. Nf6 for black. This has the benefit of keeping the plans in spirit of the French Defense. The main advantage the Tarrasche has over the Classical variations is that the knight on d2 does not get in the way of the c pawn. Additionally, White's kingside knight should go to d2 instead of the more active f3 square to get out of the way of the f pawn so it may go to f4 and f5. Additionally, the knight on d2 can go to f3 instead so that both knights defend the d4 pawn. This happens after black plays Qb6. Black also has access to the move f6, which is part of the mainline. White can either capture The pawn or play Nf4, which I have omitted from the visual due to its tricky nature.

Burn and Rubenstein Variations

These two variations are less common, and they can reach similar positions so I have combined them. There are some differences depending on move orders that can lead to different ideas. The diagram shows the similar nature of the two openings. These lines do not follow the typical French Defense plans as the positions are very open. There is also the Morozevich Line, characterized by gxf6. It and similar gxf6 lines can lead to tactical games.

Exchange Variation

The Exchange Variation is initially very symmetrical, thus both sides can adopt the same types of plans. The diagram shows some of the various setups that can be achieved for both sides. Other than that, the position is easy to play and can as symmetrical as you want it to be.
